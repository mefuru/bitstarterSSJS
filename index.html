At University, you're given a class timetable on your first day. You know where you need to be, at what time and for how long. Although it can be a burden to attend a lecture at 9am on a Friday morning, the timetable provides a helpful structure.

I have found that without a structured learning schedule, a disciplined approach can be difficult (especially if you're studying multiple MOOCs simutaneously, have a job or other commitments). Sometimes, I've had to rush through lectures and assignments to submit on time - this defeats to purpose of MOOCs. MOOC Timetable integrates into your calendar, around your free time.

There are three steps to receiving your free MOOC Timetable

  1. Login with your Gmail calendar
  2. Give us a list of your current and planned MOOCs
  3. Add personal preferences (I'm a night owl, 5am starts just won't work for me!)

We will give you a timetable for you to fit in your studies. If it seems like you've got too much on your plate, we will let you know straight away so you can plan accordingl. Timetables fits around existing events in your calendar - we will only schedule time in free time.

FAQ

What is a MOOC?
A MOOC is an online course aimed at large-scale interactive participation and open access via the web. In addition to traditional course materials such as videos, readings, and problem sets, MOOCs provide interactive user forums that help build a community for the students, professors, and teaching assistants. (Source: Wikipedia)
Where can I take MOOCs?
Check out coursera.org, www.udacity.com, edxonline.org and venture-lab.org for an excellent selection of MOOCs. MOOC Timetable is targeted towards courses which have a strict schedule and deadlines. Udacity, for example, has courses that you can do whenever you want so you can manage your own time, whereas Coursera courses have strict deadlines for assignments and MOOC Timetable would be useful.
